the average person does not exist

An average is a compression, not a human. It works fine for individual traits. Stack enough of them together and the person disappears. This is not a rounding error. It is the math.

standard deviation ~8 min read crowd simulator · jagged profile builder · distribution explorer subscribe

There is a person we keep designing the world around. They have an average commute, an average income, an average body, an average household size, an average reaction time, an average retirement balance, and an average tolerance for the systems supposedly built for people like them. This person is in every policy model, school calendar, car seat, building code, and budget template. They are everywhere. They do not exist.

Or rather — they exist only as math. An average is not a human being. It is a compression. A way of taking a messy cloud of variation and replacing it with one clean number that feels more solid than it is. Sometimes that is useful. But averages have a habit of pretending to be normal. And normal is where things get strange.

In the 1950s, the U.S. Air Force designed its cockpits around the "average" pilot's body — average height, arm length, torso, leg reach. Then they looked at how many pilots actually fell near average across all those dimensions simultaneously. The answer was close to zero. The cockpit had been built for a body that almost nobody had. The average was mathematically accurate. It just wasn't real.

This is the trap. Averages can describe groups while failing individuals. A dataset's center point tells you where the middle of the crowd is. It does not tell you where any particular person stands — or whether anyone stands there at all.

The reason is that people are jagged. You can be above average in one dimension, below in another, completely typical in a third. Someone can have average income but unusually high medical expenses. Average height but unusually short arms. Average commute but an unusually unreliable bus. The "average person" flattens all of that into a line. But the landscape is not flat.

The math here is not subtle. If you define "average" as falling in the middle 50% for any single trait, roughly half the population qualifies. Add a second independent trait and it is about 25%. A third: 12.5%. By the time you stack seven or eight traits together, you are looking for someone in the neighborhood of 1% of the population — and that is with generous definitions of "average." The Air Force was using narrower bands and came up with zero.

This matters because systems are designed from the center outward. A school day assumes a certain kind of household. A work schedule assumes a certain kind of body and commute. A tax form assumes a certain kind of family structure. A budget app assumes a certain kind of paycheck. And when people do not fit, we call them edge cases — as if the middle were the only real place to be.

What if the edge cases are the population? What if being "not average" is the most normal thing about most people?

The design implication of this is uncomfortable. If we cannot find an average person to build for, we have two options: build for the most common individual cases separately, which is expensive; or build for a range of variation, which requires accepting that the system will not be optimal for anyone in particular. Most institutions choose a third option: build for the center and call everyone else an exception.

The cockpit story ended well, eventually. The Air Force's solution was adjustable seats, adjustable pedals, adjustable equipment — designing for the range instead of the point. That reframing — "how much variation can this system handle?" instead of "what does the average user want?" — is the better question. It is also a harder one, because it requires admitting that the average was never really there.
