where your electricity comes from and what it costs the planet

Vermont generates electricity at 18 grams of CO₂ per kilowatt-hour. West Virginia generates it at 818. They pay similar prices. The difference is entirely where you live and what fuel the grid decided to build its infrastructure around fifty years ago.

When you plug something into the wall, the electrons that flow through it were generated somewhere — a coal plant in Wyoming, a hydroelectric dam in Oregon, a nuclear reactor in Illinois, a solar farm in California, a wind turbine in Iowa, or more likely some combination of all of the above drawn from a regional grid that balances supply and demand in real time across multiple states. The outlet doesn't know and doesn't tell you. The carbon intensity of those electrons varies by roughly a factor of 45 depending on where in the United States you live.

West Virginia and Wyoming get roughly two-thirds of their electricity from coal. Vermont gets essentially none from coal and runs almost entirely on a combination of nuclear, hydroelectric, wind, solar, and biomass. Vermont's grid is one of the cleanest in the developed world — comparable to France, which runs heavily on nuclear. West Virginia's is dirtier than China's average. Both grids deliver electrons at roughly similar retail prices. The difference is not primarily about cost. It is about what was built, when, and by whom, and about the political economy of an industry that does not naturally replace itself.

The US electricity grid is not one thing. It is a patchwork of regional grids, each with its own generation mix, its own regulatory environment, and its own carbon footprint — connected to its neighbors in some places and not in others. Charging your electric vehicle in Idaho costs the climate roughly the same as not driving at all. Charging it in West Virginia costs more than a gas car.

The economics of the electricity grid are path-dependent in a way that makes rapid transformation difficult. A coal plant built in 1970 and paid off by 1990 produces electricity at extremely low marginal cost. The capital is already sunk. The fuel is cheap. There is no financial incentive for the utility to replace it with something cleaner until it either breaks down or is regulated out of existence. Meanwhile, the transmission infrastructure, the workforce skills, the supply chains, and the regulatory relationships are all calibrated to coal. Transitioning is not just a matter of building new generation — it requires unwinding a half-century of complementary investments.

The renewable energy story is geographically determined in a way that often gets lost in policy discussions. Iowa gets nearly 60% of its electricity from wind — not because Iowa has an unusually progressive energy policy, but because Iowa has extremely consistent wind at the right height and the economics of wind power have made it the cheapest source of new generation available. South Dakota gets 48% from wind for the same reason. Kansas gets 44%. These are not politically liberal states. They are states with good wind resources that made the economic calculation available to every utility.

Hydroelectric power tells a similar story of geographic determinism. Oregon gets 48% of its electricity from hydro. Washington gets 68%. Idaho gets 68%. These three states have some of the cleanest and cheapest electricity in the country — not by choice but by geography. The Columbia River system produces enormous amounts of dispatchable, zero-carbon electricity, and has done so since the 1930s. The Bonneville Power Administration, which manages that system, has delivered electricity to the Pacific Northwest for nearly a century at prices that are among the lowest in the nation.

Vermont and South Dakota have the lowest carbon intensity. Hawaii has the highest retail price. High-carbon states are not consistently cheaper or more expensive than low-carbon states.
The scatter reveals something counterintuitive: dirty electricity is not consistently cheaper than clean. Hawaii is expensive and dirty. Idaho is cheap and clean. The price is driven by geography and infrastructure history, not carbon content.

The scatterplot makes visible the thing that is hardest to communicate about electricity policy: there is no simple tradeoff between cheap and clean. Hawaii is the most expensive state for electricity — over 40 cents per kilowatt-hour — and it is also among the most carbon-intensive, because almost all of its fuel arrives by ship. Idaho is among the cheapest states and among the cleanest, because of the Columbia River system. West Virginia has cheap electricity and extremely dirty electricity. Massachusetts has expensive electricity and moderately clean electricity. The variables do not move together in any consistent direction.

Nuclear power is the variable that most consistently produces low-carbon electricity at moderate prices in states that don't have favorable geography for renewables. Illinois, which gets 54% of its electricity from nuclear, has a carbon intensity roughly comparable to France. Connecticut, South Carolina, and New Hampshire all have significant nuclear capacity and correspondingly lower carbon intensity than their geography would otherwise allow. The political challenge for nuclear is not primarily economic — it is the combination of regulatory complexity, public perception shaped by decades of opposition, and the 15–20 year timeline from decision to operation that makes it difficult to respond to the speed of the current energy transition.

Iowa produces more wind energy than it consumes. It exports the surplus to neighboring states. This is not in Iowa's political messaging about energy policy, but it is in the data — and it suggests that the geography of American renewable energy production does not map neatly onto the geography of American climate politics.
