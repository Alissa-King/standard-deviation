scale & perception · vol. 04

your brain is bad at big numbers. here's the evidence.

A magnitude 9 earthquake releases 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 7. Not twice as much. 1,000 times. The scales we use to measure things are quietly hiding the truth in plain sight.

standard deviation ~8 min read toggle: log vs. linear subscribe

The Richter scale runs from 1 to 10. This makes it sound like a reasonable, human-sized thing — a scale you could hang on a wall like a poster, one that you could understand by looking at. It is not that. The Richter scale is logarithmic, which means every whole number represents a 10-fold increase in amplitude and roughly a 31.6-fold increase in energy released. A magnitude 5 earthquake releases about the same energy as a nuclear bomb. A magnitude 9 releases roughly 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 7. The scale looks like it spans ten steps. It spans something closer to a billion.

This is not a quirk of seismology. Almost every scale humans use to measure extreme phenomena is logarithmic, because the phenomena themselves span ranges too vast for a linear scale to represent legibly. Sound is measured in decibels — logarithmic. Acidity is measured in pH — logarithmic. Stellar brightness is measured in magnitudes — logarithmic, and confusingly reversed so that brighter stars have smaller numbers. The human ear, the human eye, and it turns out the human sense of quantity are all themselves roughly logarithmic, which is probably why we keep reaching for these scales. They feel natural. They are also, consistently, hiding from us how extreme the extremes actually are.

The problem with logarithmic scales is not that they are wrong. They are often the most honest representation of the data. The problem is that we read the numbers and our brain treats them as if they were linear — as if 8 were twice 4, instead of being a hundred times larger.

The best way to understand what a log scale is hiding is to remove it. The charts below let you toggle between the scale as it's conventionally presented and the underlying linear reality. Some of them will break something in your perception of the thing being measured. That's the point.

01 — earthquake energy: what the Richter scale is hiding
showing: richter magnitude (logarithmic)
On the log scale, a M9 looks about 29% larger than a M7. Switch to linear to see the actual energy difference: 1,000×. The 1906 San Francisco earthquake essentially disappears.

The 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami — the one that triggered the Fukushima disaster — was a magnitude 9.1. The 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake that collapsed a section of the Bay Bridge during the World Series was a magnitude 6.9. On the Richter scale, 9.1 and 6.9 look reasonably close. The actual energy difference is somewhere in the range of 250,000 to 1. Tōhoku released about 600 million times as much energy as a magnitude 4 earthquake. You've felt a magnitude 4. It rattles the windows.

Sound works the same way. Zero decibels is roughly the threshold of human hearing — the quietest sound a healthy young person can detect. 60 decibels is a normal conversation. 120 decibels is a jet engine at close range and is the approximate pain threshold for human hearing. This sounds like a range of 120. It is actually a range of one trillion in sound pressure. The difference between a whisper and a jet engine, in actual pressure on your eardrum, is a factor of 10 billion.

02 — sound pressure: what decibels are hiding
showing: decibels (logarithmic)
On the dB scale a jet engine looks 12× louder than a whisper. In actual pressure: 10,000,000,000×. Every 20 dB is a 10× increase in pressure.

The national debt works on a similar perceptual problem, except without the benefit of a scale name that signals logarithm. A million dollars, a billion dollars, and a trillion dollars look like the same kind of number — they're just different words with the same rhythm. They are not the same kind of number. A million seconds is about 11.5 days. A billion seconds is about 31.7 years. A trillion seconds is about 31,688 years — further back in time than the oldest known cave paintings. The words are close. The things they describe are not.

If you spent a dollar every second, it would take you 11.5 days to spend a million dollars. 31.7 years to spend a billion. And 31,688 years to spend a trillion. The US national debt, at roughly $36 trillion, would take you approximately 1.14 million years to spend at that rate.
03 — million, billion, trillion: the scale you already know wrong
showing: log scale
On log scale, a billion looks about twice a million. Switch to linear: a billion is 1,000 millions. The US national debt doesn't fit on this chart at linear scale unless everything else is invisible.

pH is perhaps the most useful everyday example because it's both logarithmic and reversed, which means it manages to be counterintuitive in two directions simultaneously. Pure water has a pH of 7. Battery acid has a pH of around 0. Bleach has a pH of around 13. Each whole number step represents a 10-fold change in hydrogen ion concentration. Black coffee, at pH 5, is 100 times more acidic than pure water. Lemon juice, at pH 2, is 100,000 times more acidic than water. These foods sit next to each other at every breakfast table and their acidity differs by a factor of 100,000.

There is a version of this for stellar brightness, for the frequency range of the electromagnetic spectrum, for the size difference between a proton and a basketball, for the age of the universe compared to recorded human history. In each case, the conventional representation hides the actual scale behind a compact and legible number. The number is not lying. It is simply doing what logarithms do — compressing an incomprehensible range into something that fits on a label — and we keep forgetting to uncompress it in our heads.
