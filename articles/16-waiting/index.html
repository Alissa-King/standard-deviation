how much of your life is just waiting

Red lights. Hold music. Loading bars. Appointment delays. Waiting arrives in crumbs too small to mourn. But crumbs accumulate. At some point a life contains a second life made entirely of waiting.

Waiting rarely feels like a life event. It comes in pieces too small to mourn. Three minutes at a red light. Seven minutes in line. Twelve minutes on hold. Four minutes for the microwave. Six minutes for the doctor to come in. Nine minutes for the prescription. Twenty-two minutes refreshing a tracking page. Thirty seconds for the elevator. Two minutes for the file to upload. Eight minutes because someone is late. One minute because the app is thinking.

Waiting does not usually arrive as a catastrophe. It arrives as crumbs. But crumbs accumulate. And at some point, a life contains a second life made entirely of waiting.

Small units of time are psychologically slippery. Five minutes feels like nothing. Five minutes a day is more than thirty hours a year. Thirty minutes a day is more than a full week of waking life. This is why waiting is hard to see: it is not usually dramatic enough to trigger a decision. It is just irritating enough to make you check your phone — which makes the time disappear without coming back.

The commute is one of the few kinds of waiting we actually measure. The US Census reported a mean one-way travel time of 27.2 minutes in 2024 — 54 minutes round trip, 272 minutes a week, roughly 226 hours a year, or more than nine full 24-hour days. Just commuting. Before the school pickup line, the pharmacy, the DMV, the software update, the appointment that starts late, and the delivery window that says "8 a.m. to 5 p.m." like that is a reasonable way to describe time.

The phone has made this invisible. Not because the time returned. Because we stopped noticing it leave.

Not all waiting is equal. A wealthy person waits differently than a poor one. A salaried employee waits differently than an hourly worker. Someone with childcare waits differently than someone without it. Someone with a car waits differently than someone dependent on an infrequent bus. Someone with paid leave waits differently than someone who loses wages for every medical appointment.

Time is not just personal. It is political. A long line is sometimes a staffing choice. A three-hour appointment window is sometimes a monopoly flex. A slow benefits office is sometimes an administrative burden dressed as a process. An unreliable bus route is sometimes a budget priority wearing a schedule. A phone tree is sometimes a wall — one designed to exhaust the caller rather than assist them.

Not all waiting is waste. Some waiting is restful. Some is social. Some is the price of living among other people. But some waiting is imposed — bad design, understaffing, poverty, bureaucracy. The distinction matters: chosen waiting is a life. Imposed waiting is a tax paid in minutes by people who can least afford it.

There is also a class dimension to the commute number that the average obscures. The 27-minute mean hides an enormous range. High-income workers disproportionately work from home or near where they live. Low-income workers often live farther from their jobs because housing near employment centers is expensive, and take longer routes because transit coverage is worse in underserved areas. The people whose time is most economically constrained are often the ones spending the most of it in transit.

A nine-hour annualized commute burden, distributed unequally, is not a commuting problem. It is a housing and transportation problem that shows up in commuting data.
