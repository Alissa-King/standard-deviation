the sound that's shortening your life

The WHO estimates that long-term traffic noise causes 1.6 million healthy life-years lost annually in Western Europe alone. There is a dose-response relationship between nighttime noise and cardiovascular mortality that is as well-established as the smoking-cancer link. The geography is not random.

The connection between loud noise and hearing damage is obvious and well-known. The connection between chronic moderate noise and cardiovascular mortality is less obvious, less known, and considerably better established than most people realize.

The mechanism is not hearing damage. The mechanism is stress. Noise activates the sympathetic nervous system — the fight-or-flight response — even during sleep, even when you are not consciously aware of it. Your body registers a sound as a potential threat, releases cortisol and adrenaline, raises your blood pressure, and increases your heart rate. During waking hours, you can habituate to chronic noise and the physiological response diminishes somewhat. During sleep, habituation is incomplete. The body's stress response to noise is preserved even when the conscious mind is not registering the sound at all.

Nighttime noise is more damaging per decibel than daytime noise, because the body cannot fully suppress its physiological response during sleep. A bedroom window facing a moderately busy street, at 55 dB at night, produces cardiovascular risk comparable to years of mild hypertension.

The dose-response relationship between nighttime road traffic noise and cardiovascular disease has been documented in multiple large prospective cohort studies. The most comprehensive meta-analysis, by Babisch (2014), found that for every 10 dB increase in nighttime road traffic noise above 50 dB, the relative risk of coronary heart disease increased by approximately 7–17%. The European Environment Agency's subsequent estimates translated this into population-level burden: 1.6 million disability-adjusted life-years (DALYs) lost annually in the European Union from traffic noise alone, making it the second-largest environmental health risk after air pollution. The effect is not small. It is comparable in scale to the health burden of passive smoking.

01 — the dose-response curve: noise and cardiovascular risk
55 dB
The WHO nighttime noise guideline for outdoor areas is 40 dB Lnight. Most urban residents in Europe and North America experience 50–65 dB. The curve shows relative risk — 1.0 is baseline.

The geography of noise exposure is not random, and this is where the public health implications get pointed. The neighborhoods with the highest noise exposure are, systematically, not the neighborhoods where decisions about transportation infrastructure are made. Highway placement decisions in the United States, made largely in the mid-twentieth century, followed a pattern documented by urban historians: highways were routed through neighborhoods with less political power to resist them. Those neighborhoods are disproportionately lower-income and disproportionately non-white. Fifty years later, those same communities bear disproportionate burdens of noise-related cardiovascular disease, childhood cognitive impacts from traffic noise, and reduced property values that limit their ability to mitigate the exposure through soundproofing or relocation.

Children are particularly vulnerable. A series of studies beginning with Stansfeld et al. (2005) documented cognitive impacts from chronic aircraft noise exposure in schoolchildren: reading comprehension, memory, and sustained attention all showed dose-dependent impairment at noise levels common near airports and highways. A child whose school is under an airport flight path or adjacent to a highway is operating under a chronic, measurable cognitive handicap that is invisible in their test scores until you look at the noise data.

The solutions are known. Sound barriers reduce highway noise by 5–15 dB at adjacent properties. Low-noise road surfaces reduce rolling noise by 3–8 dB. Building setbacks, urban form regulations, and quiet-side orientation in building design can reduce exposure in new construction. The problem is not technical — it is economic and political. Sound barriers cost money. Low-noise surfaces cost more than conventional asphalt. Quiet-side orientation reduces the density achievable on a given footprint. And the communities that bear the greatest noise burden are typically the least equipped to advocate for the capital investment required to address it.

There is also a straightforward intervention that requires no infrastructure at all: telling people what their noise exposure is and what it means. The health effects of noise are not well understood by the public, and the framing of noise as an annoyance rather than a health hazard shapes both individual and policy responses. A person who understands that their 62 dB bedroom window is increasing their cardiovascular risk by 15–20% relative to a quieter option is better equipped to make decisions about where to live, how to advocate for noise mitigation in their neighborhood, and what to ask of their doctor, than a person who just knows the street is loud.

Noise is invisible in the way that air pollution was invisible before we started measuring it. The health effects accumulated quietly for decades before the epidemiological literature reached the scale needed to make the dose-response relationship undeniable. We are roughly twenty years behind on noise where we were with particulate matter in 1990.
