what actually happens to your recycling

You rinse the jar. You put it in the blue bin. After that, the story gets much less comforting. Recycling is not a promise. It is a market with a morality costume.

standard deviation ~8 min read wishcycling sorter · Sankey flow · material confidence meter subscribe

Recycling is one of those household rituals that feels like moral closure. You rinse the jar. Flatten the box. Check the little triangle. Put the thing in the blue bin instead of the trash. The lid closes. The truck comes. The object disappears. And because it disappeared through the correct container, it feels like the story ended well.

But the blue bin is not an ending. It is a sorting question. Everything in that bin still has to survive a trip from your kitchen to a truck, from the truck to a facility, from the facility to a bale, from the bale to a buyer, from the buyer to a manufacturing process, and from that process into something worth making again. At every step, things fall out. Some materials are valuable. Some are technically recyclable but not economically worth recycling. Some are contaminated. Some have no buyer. Some get downcycled. Some end up landfilled anyway.

There is a word for putting something in the recycling bin because you hope it is recyclable: wishcycling. It is such a perfect term because it captures the emotional logic of the blue bin. We are not just sorting materials. We are sorting guilt.

Recycling systems do not run on hope. They run on machinery, labor, resale value, local contracts, contamination thresholds, and commodity markets. The national recycling and composting rate in the US has hovered around 32% for years — a third of municipal solid waste diverted from landfill. That is not nothing. It is also not what the blue bin promises at the curb.

The material matters enormously. Paper is different from aluminum. Aluminum is different from glass. Glass is different from plastic. Plastic is not even one thing — it is a family of materials with different chemistries, melting points, markets, and contamination tolerances. The triangle symbol on packaging may be one of the most misleading design elements in modern life. It looks like a promise. Often it is just a resin code.

Contamination is the number that matters most and gets discussed the least. A single contaminated item — a greasy pizza box in the paper stream, a food-coated container in the plastic bale, a broken drinking glass in the glass bin — can contaminate an entire load. Some facilities reject whole truckloads over a contamination threshold. The economics of recycling are thin enough that even a small degradation in material quality can push a bale from "valuable commodity" to "not worth processing."

This is why plastic bags are the recycling program's particular nemesis. They are technically recyclable at certain drop-off locations. But in a curbside bin, they wind around sorting machinery at materials recovery facilities and shut the operation down. Curbside programs across the country have sent increasingly direct instructions about plastic bags. The bags keep arriving anyway, full of recyclables, because people's intentions are good and the information never quite reaches the moment of decision.

After China stopped accepting most foreign recyclables in 2018 — the National Sword policy, which set strict contamination limits — US recycling programs faced a crisis. Material that had been shipped to Chinese processors for decades suddenly had no buyer. Cities and counties faced the choice of paying to landfill material that had previously been sold, or shutting programs down entirely. Many did both. The cost of recycling to municipalities roughly tripled overnight in some regions. Some programs have recovered as domestic processing capacity has grown. Others have narrowed what they accept. A few have not recovered at all.

None of that appears on the blue bin.
