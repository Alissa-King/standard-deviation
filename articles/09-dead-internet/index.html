how much of the internet is real

A measurable percentage of web traffic is bots. A measurable percentage of social followers are fake. A measurable percentage of product reviews are fabricated. The numbers have been estimated. They are worse than you assumed.

In 2023, Imperva released its annual Bad Bot Report, which estimated that 49.6% of all internet traffic was automated — bots, scrapers, crawlers, account checkers, credential stuffers, and other non-human agents. For the first time in the report's history, automated traffic exceeded human traffic. This number requires interpretation: not all bots are malicious (search engine crawlers are bots; web monitors are bots; the thing that generates your link preview is a bot), and Imperva has an obvious commercial interest in alarming estimates. But even the more conservative estimates from independent researchers place non-human traffic at 35–45% of the total. The internet is substantially talking to itself.

The dead internet theory — a loosely defined idea that circulated on conspiracy forums starting around 2021 — claimed that the internet had become a ghost town of AI-generated content, bot interactions, and corporate astroturfing, with genuine human activity reduced to a tiny fraction of what appears to be organic engagement. This is too dramatic as stated. But the underlying data, when you look at it, suggests a more uncomfortable version of the same concern: not that the internet is dead, but that the parts of it that look most alive are often the parts that aren't.

The engagement metrics that determine what content gets amplified — likes, shares, comments, follower counts, view counts — are all gameable, and all gamed, at scales large enough to meaningfully distort what the internet thinks is popular versus what is actually popular.

Not all bot traffic is malicious — search crawlers, monitoring tools, and link previewers are all bots. But the "bad bot" category (scrapers, credential stuffers, fake account creators) is substantial in every sector.

The follower economy is perhaps the most legible manifestation of the problem. Instagram's own internal estimates, disclosed in SEC filings from the Meta era, suggested that between 5% and 10% of monthly active accounts were fake or duplicate at any given time. External researchers, using behavioral analysis of posting patterns, engagement ratios, and account creation metadata, have estimated the figure to be higher — somewhere between 10% and 30% depending on the account type and platform. Accounts with very high follower counts and very low engagement rates are a statistical signature of purchased followers. The ratio of followers to engagement is an imperfect but measurable indicator of inauthenticity.

The review economy has its own version of this problem. A 2022 study by Fakespot, which analyzes product review authenticity, found that approximately 31% of reviews on Amazon were fake or unreliable. The percentage was higher in certain categories: electronics (45%), beauty products (52%), supplements (60%). The fake reviews are not always obviously fake. They are written by humans — via platforms like Mechanical Turk or, increasingly, by language models — following specific instructions about length, specificity, and the star rating required to maintain a seller's algorithmic ranking.

The content side of the dead internet concern has become more acute since 2022. The cost of generating text content dropped to near-zero with the proliferation of large language models, and the web has responded accordingly. Researchers at NewsGuard, which tracks unreliable news sites, identified over 1,000 websites in 2024 that were largely or entirely AI-generated — producing dozens of articles per day on topics ranging from politics to celebrity news, monetized through programmatic advertising, with no meaningful editorial oversight. The content is coherent, SEO-optimized, and devoid of the kind of original reporting or genuine perspective that would distinguish it from authentic journalism. It is readable. It is not real.

What makes this particularly difficult to measure is that the line between "automated" and "human" content has never been clear. Press release journalism has always existed. Content farms filled with low-paid human writers have always existed. The question is not whether the internet contains inauthentic content — it always has — but whether the proportion has crossed a threshold that changes how the network functions. If the content people share and engage with is substantially generated by systems optimizing for engagement rather than truth, the feedback loop between what is popular and what is real gets severed, and the mechanism by which the internet is supposed to surface good information breaks down.

The internet runs on engagement metrics. Engagement metrics are gameable. They have been gamed, at scale, for long enough that the platforms that depend on them have partially internalized the distortion and partially failed to notice it. The number of humans paying attention at any given moment is lower than every dashboard shows.
