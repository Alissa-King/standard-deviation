the West is running out of water and has been for twenty years

The megadrought in the Colorado River Basin is not a weather event. It is a structural water deficit that has been building since 2000, interrupted by a few wet years that felt like recovery and weren't. The animated map makes this visible in a way the annual headlines don't.

Lake Mead, the reservoir formed by Hoover Dam on the Colorado River, reached its full capacity in 1983. In 2022, it fell to 27 percent of capacity — the lowest level since the reservoir was first filled in 1937. The surface of the water dropped more than 170 feet from its high point. A shoreline of white calcium carbonate, called the bathtub ring, marked the former waterline on the canyon walls. Boats that had been resting on the bottom since the 1950s emerged from the receding water. Several sets of human remains emerged with them.

Lake Mead supplies drinking water to roughly 25 million people in Nevada, Arizona, and California. The Colorado River, which fills it, is also the primary water source for agriculture in the Southwest — irrigation that produces a substantial fraction of the vegetables consumed in the United States during winter months. When the Bureau of Reclamation declared the first-ever Tier 2 water shortage in August 2022, it triggered mandatory cutbacks across seven states. Those cutbacks were the first, not the last.

The drought is not one event with a start date. The US Drought Monitor has been tracking conditions weekly since 2000, and if you animate those 24 years of data, what you see is not a series of droughts. What you see is a ratchet — conditions tightening in the West, relaxing slightly in wet years, tightening further, and never returning to the baseline that the 20th century treated as normal.

The period from 2020 to 2022 is now referred to as the peak of the western megadrought — the driest 22-year period in at least 1,200 years, based on tree ring reconstructions of historical soil moisture. The word megadrought is not hyperbole. It is a technical classification for a drought lasting more than two decades, and the 2000–2022 western drought qualifies. The 2023 wet year in California partially refilled reservoirs and briefly lifted the drought classification over much of the state. The structural deficit in the Colorado River Basin — the accumulated difference between how much water the seven basin states are legally entitled to use and how much the river actually produces in an era of warming temperatures and reduced snowpack — did not go away.

The Colorado River Compact of 1929 apportioned 16.5 million acre-feet of water per year among seven states and Mexico. The river actually produces, in recent decades, roughly 12 to 14 million acre-feet per year. The compact was negotiated during one of the wettest periods in the river's recorded history. The legal framework for western water allocation was built on a baseline that no longer exists.

Each cell is one state × one year. The color shows peak drought severity reached that year. 2021 stands out: every western state simultaneously reached extreme to exceptional drought — something that had not happened in the observational record.

The heatmap shows what the annual news cycle obscures: the drought is not episodic. California went from exceptional drought in 2014 to near-normal conditions in 2017 to severe drought again in 2020 to exceptional drought in 2021 to a wet 2023 that provided temporary relief. If you read each of those transitions as a separate story, you miss the arc. The arc is a decline interrupted by recoveries that are getting shorter and less complete.

Temperature is the factor that distinguishes the current period from historical droughts of similar precipitation deficit. Higher temperatures increase evaporation from reservoirs, increase transpiration from plants and soil, and accelerate snowpack melt — which shifts water availability from spring and summer, when it's needed for agriculture, to winter, when the infrastructure isn't designed to capture it. The same amount of precipitation produces less available water at higher temperatures. The Colorado River Basin has warmed approximately 1.5°C since 1970. Climate models project another 1.5–2°C of warming by mid-century under moderate emissions scenarios. The water math gets worse in each of those scenarios, not better.

The bathtub ring on the canyon walls at Lake Mead is the most visible data point in American climate science. It requires no statistical training to read. It is 170 feet of white calcium carbonate on red sandstone, and it says: this is how much water is gone.
