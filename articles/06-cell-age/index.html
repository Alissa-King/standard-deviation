how old is your body, really

Your taste buds are 10 days old. Your red blood cells are 4 months old. Your skeleton is about 10 years old. Your cortical neurons are as old as you are. Enter your age and find out which parts of you are practically newborn.

standard deviation ~8 min read data: Frisén lab, radiocarbon dating studies subscribe

Somewhere in the early 2000s, a Swedish biologist named Jonas Frisén had an unusual idea. The Cold War had ended, atmospheric nuclear testing had stopped, and the pulse of radioactive carbon-14 that the tests had injected into the atmosphere — the "bomb pulse" — was slowly decaying back to background levels. But Frisén realized that every cell in every human body alive during those tests had incorporated some of that carbon-14 into its DNA when it divided. And because C-14 decays at a known rate, and because the atmospheric concentration at any given year is known from ice core records, you could in principle look at the carbon-14 in a cell's DNA and calculate, with reasonable precision, when that cell was born.

What Frisén's lab found when they started applying this technique to human tissue samples was that the body is not one age. It is many ages, layered on top of each other, each layer turning over on its own schedule, some of them replacing themselves constantly and others lasting for decades without replacement.

The Ship of Theseus problem — if you replace every plank in a ship, is it still the same ship? — turns out to have a precise biological answer, and the answer is: it depends which plank you mean, and how long you wait.

Your gut lining renews itself every 2 to 4 days. The cells lining your intestines divide constantly and are shed constantly — the entire inner surface of your digestive tract is replaced roughly every week. Your taste buds, which are specialized epithelial cells on the surface of your tongue, turn over every 10 days or so. Your skin replaces its outer layers over about two to four weeks. Your red blood cells — the most numerous cells in your body — live for roughly 120 days before being broken down in the spleen. These are the cells of constant renewal, the high-turnover parts of you that are perpetually almost brand new.

Further down the turnover timescale sit your fat cells, which replace themselves at a rate of about 10% per year — so the average fat cell in your body is roughly a decade old, though that average conceals significant variation. Your muscle cells are more stable than most people think: the nuclei of skeletal muscle cells are rarely replaced in healthy adults, and the average age of a muscle nucleus in a sedentary middle-aged person is around 15 years. Your bone cells — osteoblasts and osteoclasts — participate in a continuous remodeling process that replaces most of the skeleton roughly every 10 years.

And then there are the neurons. The cells of the cerebral cortex — the outer layer of the brain responsible for conscious thought, language, memory, and personality — are, in the vast majority of cases, as old as you are. They were born during fetal development or very shortly after birth, and they do not divide again. The neurons you are using to read this sentence were present when you were learning to walk. A small number of brain regions, notably the olfactory bulb and parts of the hippocampus, do generate new neurons throughout life — but the cortex is essentially static at the cellular level. The cells change; the connections between them change constantly; the cells themselves do not.

This is not a flaw. The neuroscientific consensus is that the non-renewal of cortical neurons is probably deliberate — that replacing neurons would disrupt the encoded information in the synaptic connections between them, and that the stability of the cells is what allows long-term memory to persist over decades. Your oldest memories are stored in connections between cells that have existed since you were an infant. The cells that remember your childhood are still your original cells.

You are a palimpsest in the same sense as the night sky. The different parts of you are from different times. The version of you that is reading this sentence contains cells that are days old and cells that are decades old, running simultaneously, with no obvious seam between them.

The heart sits in an interesting middle position. For a long time, cardiologists believed that heart muscle cells — cardiomyocytes — did not renew at all, which is why heart attacks cause permanent damage. Frisén's bomb-pulse technique revised this picture. The heart does replace cardiomyocytes, but very slowly — about 1% per year when you're young, slowing further as you age. A 40-year-old's cardiomyocytes are, on average, a mix: some original cells from birth, some renewed in the past decade. The overall average age of a 40-year-old's heart muscle cells works out to roughly 6 to 10 years, which is younger than the heart itself but far older than the stomach lining.

The lens of the eye is perhaps the most extreme case of non-renewal. The proteins in the center of the eye lens — the crystallins — are synthesized during fetal development and never replaced. They are, in a person in their 70s, 70 years old. This is part of why the lens progressively stiffens and yellows with age, and why cataracts form: the proteins have simply been around too long, and the accumulated oxidative damage is not repaired because there is no replacement process. Your eye lens is the oldest intact structure in your body. It is older than the cortex, which at least maintains its cellular machinery. The crystallins in the center of your lens are the same molecules that were there at birth.

The weighted average age of all the cells in your body — what you might call your cellular age — is substantially lower than your chronological age. The exact number depends on how you weight different cell types, but estimates for a 40-year-old human consistently come out somewhere in the range of 7 to 10 years. The high-turnover tissues drag the average down. You are, in aggregate cellular terms, younger than you think.

This is not a consolation. It just means the Ship of Theseus problem is more interesting than a simple yes or no. Most of the ship has been replaced. Some of the original planks are still there and cannot be replaced without losing something. The question of whether it's the same ship is not a question about the planks — it's a question about what you think ships are for.

what this made me wonder next
If cortical neurons don't replace themselves, what actually changes in the brain over a lifetime — and what does that mean for memory, personality, and the self?
Why does the eye lens accumulate damage while other tissues renew? What would have to change evolutionarily for lens proteins to be replaced?
Cancer cells that have been in culture since the 1950s — HeLa cells, taken from Henrietta Lacks — are still dividing. In what sense are they still alive? In what sense are they still her?
