the Big One is overdue. here's what that actually means.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone hasn't ruptured in over 320 years. We know the recurrence intervals. We can calculate the probability. The number is not reassuring.

standard deviation ~10 min read data: USGS, PNSN, paleoseismic records subscribe

On January 26, 1700, at approximately nine o'clock in the evening, the Cascadia Subduction Zone ruptured along its full 700-mile length. The earthquake was magnitude 9.0 or greater. The shaking lasted several minutes. The tsunami that followed crossed the Pacific and struck Japan with enough force that Japanese officials recorded the precise arrival time, which is how we know the date at all — there was nobody left on the Pacific Northwest coast to write it down.

That was 326 years ago. The question of when it happens again is not academic.

Cascadia is not like most fault systems. It doesn't produce small earthquakes that release pressure gradually. It locks, loads, and eventually lets go all at once — and "all at once" means a rupture zone the length of California.

The fault runs from northern California to southern British Columbia, where the Juan de Fuca tectonic plate slides under the North American plate at roughly 40 millimeters per year. The plates are currently locked together across most of that interface, which means the stress is accumulating without release. Every year that passes without a rupture is another year of strain stored in the rock.

Paleoseismic records — evidence from drowned forests, tsunami deposits, and turbidite layers on the ocean floor — give us a picture of how Cascadia has behaved over the past 10,000 years. It has not behaved the same way twice. The gaps between major ruptures range from 200 to over 1,000 years. The average is somewhere around 240 years for full-margin events. We are at 326 years.

The phrase "overdue" is one seismologists tend to avoid, because it implies the fault is on a clock, which it isn't. The recurrence intervals are not regular. The next rupture could happen tomorrow or in 500 years, and the geological record doesn't distinguish between those possibilities in the way that would be useful for urban planning.

What geologists use instead is a probability calculation based on the recurrence data and a statistical model called a Poisson process — or, for faults with more regular behavior, a lognormal or Brownian Passage Time model. The math produces a probability of rupture within a given time window. It accounts for the fact that we've been waiting 326 years. It does not tell you when.

The 50-year probability of a full Cascadia rupture is currently estimated at roughly 15 percent. That sounds modest until you remember that 15 percent is also roughly the probability of rolling a 1 on a single die.

What makes Cascadia distinctively dangerous is not just the magnitude — though a full-margin rupture would be among the largest earthquakes in recorded history — but the geography. Seattle, Portland, Eugene, and most of the major population centers of the Pacific Northwest sit within the projected shaking zone. The coast faces a tsunami arrival time measured in minutes, not hours. And a significant portion of the region's critical infrastructure — bridges, highways, the electrical grid — was built before the scientific community understood what Cascadia was or what it could do.

The USGS released updated ShakeMap projections in 2022 modeling a full Cascadia rupture. The results suggested that shaking would be severe enough to cause widespread structural damage across a 700-mile corridor, with the worst outcomes concentrated in areas built on soft, saturated soils — which includes substantial portions of Seattle's SoDo district, Portland's inner east side, and nearly all of the coastal towns.

The 1700 earthquake is not the largest in Cascadia's record. Paleoseismic evidence suggests several prehistoric events may have been larger, with rupture zones extending further and tsunami deposits reaching further inland than those from 1700. The 1700 event is simply the most recent and therefore the most studied.

The question of preparedness is a separate conversation from the question of probability, and it is, frankly, a harder one. FEMA's Cascadia Rising exercises — regional disaster simulations run in 2016 and 2022 — projected that a full rupture would produce casualties and infrastructure damage that would overwhelm the emergency response capacity of every affected state simultaneously. The simulations suggested recovery timelines measured in years to decades for some areas, not weeks.

The seismologists know what's coming. The geologists know the record. The engineers know what the buildings can't survive. Whether that knowledge translates into preparation at the scale the risk demands is a policy question, which means it is also a question about attention spans and budget cycles and the difficulty of preparing for something that might not happen for another 500 years, even though it might happen Tuesday.

The fault doesn't know what year it is. It doesn't know that we built cities on top of it. It's been building pressure since 1700 and it will release that pressure when the accumulated stress exceeds the friction holding the plates together. The timing is genuinely uncertain. The event itself is not.
