a t-shirt's journey from design to landfill

Zara goes from design decision to store shelf in two weeks. The average fast fashion garment is worn seven times before disposal. Here is every step, every carbon cost, and every day elapsed between concept and landfill.

The t-shirt you are probably wearing right now began as a design decision, possibly made in Spain. The fabric was likely woven in Turkey or China from cotton grown in the United States, India, or Uzbekistan. It was cut and sewn in Bangladesh, Cambodia, or Vietnam by workers earning between $90 and $180 per month. It was shipped in a container to Rotterdam or Los Angeles, warehoused, distributed to a retail location, purchased, worn a statistically average of seven times, washed several of those times with a load that released approximately 700,000 microplastic fibers into the water system, and then donated or discarded. If donated, there is a roughly 15% chance it was resold; an 85% chance it was baled and shipped to a secondary market in Ghana, Chile, or Pakistan, where it may have been resold or dumped in a textile desert that is visible from satellite.

The entire lifecycle from design to landfill takes an average of about 14 months for a fast fashion garment. The design phase takes two weeks. The use phase takes five months if you're being generous. The garment then spends 200 years in a landfill, which is not part of the official lifecycle assessment but probably should be.

The speed of fast fashion is real and extraordinary — Zara produces about 10,000 distinct designs per year, Shein reportedly generates thousands per day. But speed is only the visible part. The hidden part is what that speed requires: a supply chain spanning four continents, labor priced below subsistence, and a product engineered to be disposable.
01 — the full supply chain: days, miles, and carbon
Each stage shows days elapsed and kg CO₂ emitted. The use phase (washing) often generates more emissions than manufacturing for garments worn many times — but fast fashion garments are worn so few times that manufacturing dominates per-wear.

The carbon footprint of a single cotton t-shirt has been studied extensively, and the estimates converge on a range of about 5–7 kg CO₂ equivalent across the full lifecycle. That number requires unpacking. About 40% of it comes from cotton cultivation — the pesticides, fertilizer, and most significantly the irrigation, since cotton is a thirsty crop that consumes about 10,000 liters of water per kilogram of fiber. About 30% comes from manufacturing, spinning, weaving, dyeing, and finishing. About 20% comes from consumer use — primarily washing and tumble drying. About 10% comes from transportation across the supply chain.

The per-wear carbon cost is where fast fashion's math breaks down completely. A garment with a 6 kg CO₂ footprint worn 200 times (a quality item worn twice a week for two years) has a per-wear cost of 30 grams. The same garment worn 7 times has a per-wear cost of 857 grams. The environmental cost is not primarily the product — it's the product's relationship to its use.

The crossover point is around 30 wears — below that, fast fashion's per-wear cost exceeds buying and wearing a higher-quality item. Above 100 wears, the use phase (washing) becomes the dominant carbon source.

The destination of discarded clothing is a story that gets less attention than it deserves. The donation economy in wealthy countries functions as a sorting mechanism: high-quality and branded secondhand items are resold domestically; everything else is baled and exported. The top destinations for used clothing exports from the United States include Ghana, Kenya, Pakistan, and Chile. Ghana's Kantamanto market in Accra receives an estimated 15 million garments per week. About 40% of them are too degraded to be sold and go directly to landfill or are burned. The rest are sorted, repaired, and sold — a labor-intensive process that employs thousands of people who are, in effect, managing the waste stream of Western consumption.

Atacama Desert in Chile has become the most visually striking symbol of this problem. Satellite images show mountains of discarded clothing — some estimates suggest tens of millions of garments — piled in the desert because there is no infrastructure to process them and they cannot biodegrade in the arid conditions. The clothes have been shipped from Europe and the United States, sorted in Iquique's free trade zone, and dumped when unsaleable. They are, in a very direct sense, the externalized waste of the fashion industry — the part of the lifecycle that doesn't appear in any brand's sustainability report.

The Atacama textile dump is visible on Google Earth. You can zoom in on coordinates 20.2°S, 70.1°W and see the mountains of clothing from space. The garments are there permanently. The desert is too dry for them to decompose. They will be there for hundreds of years.
