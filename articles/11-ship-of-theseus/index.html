what percentage of famous preserved objects is actually original

The Ship of Theseus problem — if you replace every plank in a ship, is it still the same ship? — turns out to have an empirical answer. We looked up the restoration records. Some of the answers are disturbing.

The Ship of Theseus is an ancient thought experiment: the Athenians preserved the ship that Theseus sailed on his legendary voyage, replacing planks as they rotted until, eventually, every plank had been replaced. Is it still the same ship? Plutarch posed this question around 75 CE, and it has been a fixture of philosophy ever since. The usual framing is abstract — it is a puzzle about identity and continuity over time.

The empirical version of the question is more interesting. We actually do preserve ships, buildings, tools, artworks, and vehicles over centuries. We have records — sometimes meticulous ones — of what was replaced, when, and with what. The question "what percentage of this famous object is original?" has an answer, and in many cases, that answer is not what the preservation label suggests.

The USS Constitution, docked in Boston Harbor and advertised as the world's oldest commissioned naval vessel still afloat, contains approximately 10–15% original timber. The rest has been replaced, some components multiple times. This is not a secret — the restoration records are public — but it is not prominently disclosed either.

The Temple of Ise in Japan represents the clearest case of intentional Ship of Theseus behavior. The shrine complex has been ritually dismantled and rebuilt every 20 years for approximately 1,300 years. The current structure was completed in 2013. It is, materially speaking, 13 years old. It is also considered one of the most sacred and ancient religious sites in Japan, with a history spanning 62 rebuildings. The Shinto tradition holds that it is the same sacred space regardless of the physical material — that continuity of ritual and location matters more than continuity of matter. This is a philosophical position, not a preservation error. The Japanese have thought about the Ship of Theseus for longer than most Western philosophers.

The question becomes more complicated when applied to artworks. Oil paintings are perhaps the most studied case. A painting that has been cleaned, revarnished, relined (backing canvas replaced), and inpainted (areas of lost original paint filled in by later restorers) multiple times may contain substantial non-original material that is invisible under normal viewing conditions. X-ray fluorescence analysis has shown that many famous paintings have been overpainted — sometimes by restorers trying to repair damage, sometimes by later hands trying to update the work, and occasionally by the original artist changing their mind. The "original" painting and the visible painting may not be the same thing.

The Lincoln Continental in which President Kennedy was assassinated — officially known as SS-100-X — was rebuilt so extensively after the assassination that it contains essentially none of the original body panels. The floor, the roof, the seats, the interior, the top mechanism, and most of the frame were replaced. The Secret Service kept the vehicle in service (with a different security configuration) until 1977. It is now in the Henry Ford Museum in Dearborn, Michigan, displayed as a historical artifact. What is being preserved, precisely, is a matter of interpretation.

The Cutty Sark, the famous clipper ship docked in Greenwich, London, presents an unusual case. A fire in 2007 destroyed much of what remained of the original hull planking. The subsequent restoration, completed in 2012, replaced damaged timber with new wood of matching species and used modern structural techniques to support the hull. Approximately 30% of the original timber survived the fire and the previous centuries of restoration; the rest is contemporary. The Cutty Sark is now presented, accurately, as a preserved historic vessel — but what is preserved is more accurately the form and location than the material.

Stonehenge is a case where the restoration percentage is surprisingly low and the fact is surprisingly underreported. Multiple restorations in the twentieth century — in 1901, 1919, 1958, 1959, 1964, and 1965 — re-erected fallen and leaning stones, used concrete to stabilize others, and in some cases repositioned stones whose original placement was uncertain. The site looks ancient because the stones themselves are ancient. Whether they are arranged as they were originally arranged is a more complicated question, and one that Stonehenge's official interpretive materials tend not to dwell on.

The question is not whether preservation is worthwhile. It obviously is. The question is what exactly is being preserved, and whether the story told about the object matches what the restoration records show. In most cases, there is a gap between the two.
